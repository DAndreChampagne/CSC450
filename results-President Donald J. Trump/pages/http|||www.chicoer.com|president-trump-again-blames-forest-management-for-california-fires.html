By John Woolfolk | Bay Area News Group
PUBLISHED: November 17, 2018 at 5:39 am | UPDATED: November 17, 2018 at 3:16 pm

President Donald Trump was greeted mostly by friendly supporters as he arrived in Butte County Saturday morning to visit the scene of the state’s deadliest and most destructive wildfire on record, a calamity he again blamed on forest management.

“I can’t believe he’s in Chico — it’s just wild,” said Chico State University business student Brittany Dekreek, who was among a crowd of Trump supporters along the main route from Chico to Paradise waiting to see the President pass by in his motorcade. “With all the people who are suffering right now it’s really great that he’s coming. I really love that he loves this country so much.”

Trump beat Hillary Clinton in Butte County in the 2016 election, and hundreds of people, many wearing face masks to filter out the smokey air and recording the scene on their mobile phones, lined the streets of Chico, a state college town. As the presidential motorcade drove by, some waved American flags and displayed Trump signs. A group of silent protestors held two banners that mentioned “climate change” and the “apocalypse.”

Trump arrived in Paradise around 11:30 a.m. and visited an RV park where he stopped in front of an American flag hanging from what was left of a burned structure. He walked between strewn, blackened ovens, a metal shelf with charred plant pots on top, too many burned out cars to count.

“This is very sad to see,” Trump said. “Hopefully this is going to be the last of these because this was a really, really bad one … Nobody would have ever thought this could happen.”

Despite passing a road sign warning people to “wear masks” in the Paradise area, Trump and the other elected officials didn’t don them. Officials told reporters that the air quality in the town had improved and said those with health conditions should wear them.

Trump praised firefighters, police officers and other first responders who helped residents evacuate and battled the blaze, which has claimed more than 71 lives. More than 1,000 people are still reported missing, although authorities said the list may include duplicate entries for some people.

“Law enforcement’s been beyond anything that anybody could have believed,” Trump said. “To see what’s happened here, nobody every thought this could have happened. So the federal government is behind you, we’re all behind each other.

“We have the greatest people in the world looking and helping,” he added. “Law enforcement, always. They never let us down. They never let us down.”

The President was accompanied by Gov. Jerry Brown, Lt. Governor and Governor-elect Gavin Newsom and Paradise Mayor Jody Jones.

Trump had landed shortly before 10 a.m. Saturday at Beale Air Force Base just north of Sacramento where he shook hands with Brown and Newsom on the tarmac. The Democrats are joining the president as he visits burned areas and meets with first responders. The group got onto a helicopter and flew over the scene of the still raging Camp Fire before landing in Chico an hour later, where Trump met Jones.

Retired Teamster Fred Bowerman, whose home near Paradise burned down, parked along the road and hung on the side of his truck a “Make America Great Again” flag bearing Trump’s image. The President’s visit would “bring us a little cheer and a little morale boost,” said Bowerman, 56, wearing a red MAGA hat and blue “Trump 2020” t-shirt.

Firefighters, too, felt a morale boost from the President’s visit, said Cal Fire engineer Bethany Breese, a Trump supporter from Redding.

“All through the summer we’re away from our homes, we’re away from our families. Just having that support from higher up helps keep us happy,” said Breese, 28, working at the main firefighting deployment center in Chico.

Local volunteer fire captain Roger Blancett, also a Trump supporter, said Trump’s visit would probably increase morale a bit, but that other help from the nation’s leader would be welcome.

“If he grabbed a McCloud and cut a line, that wouldn’t be bad,” said Blancette, 55, who had been fighting the Camp Fire since it started. “We’ve still got a job to do.”

President Trump had left the White House at 7:25 a.m. eastern time and spoke briefly with reporters before boarding the Marine One helicopter, according to White House pool reports.

“We will be talking about forest management,” Trump said. “I’ve been saying that for a long time. It should have been a lot different situation. But the one thing is that everybody now knows that this is what we have to be doing, and there’s no question about it. It should have been done many years ago. But I think everybody’s on the right side. It’s a big issue, it’s a big issue, a very expensive issue, but very, very inexpensive when you compare it to even one of these horrible fires. And we’ll save a lot of lives.”

The president answered questions about other topics, saying he would support Rep. Nancy Pelosi, D-San Francisco, in her expected return as House speaker, and commenting on border security, the vice president, pending appointments and funding decisions.

He then left for Joint Base Andrews, where he waved to the pool of reporters assembled on the tarmac but made no remarks as he climbed the stairs to board Air Force One for the flight to California. He is joined by aides Sarah Sanders and Dan Scavino for the trip.

On Saturday morning, Trump said on Twitter that he was being joined by representatives Kevin McCarthy, R-Bakersfield, the current House majority leader, Ken Calvert, R-Corona, and Doug LaMalfa, R-Richvale, who represents the area burned by the Camp Fire. He said he is looking forward “to being with our brave Firefighters, First Responders and FEMA, along with the many brave People of California. We are with you all the way – God Bless you all!”

This will be just Trump’s second visit to the nation’s most populous state since his election. His first California visit, which he made in March to inspect border wall prototypes outside San Diego and to attend a fundraiser in the Beverly Park home of Edward Glazer, the co-chairman of the Tampa Bay Buccaneers NFL team, came later in his term than any White House occupant since Franklin D. Roosevelt, the Los Angeles Times reported.

California overall has not been welcoming turf for the Republican president. The GOP has seen steady decline to about one in four registered California voters, has occupied no statewide office since Arnold Schwarzenegger left the governor’s office in 2011 and lost congressional seats in last week’s midterm elections. But the Camp Fire burned in the eastern part of California that remains strongly Republican — Butte County went for John Cox in the governor’s race won by Newsom.

That fire has grown to 148,000 acres and was 55 percent contained as of Saturday morning. It has claimed at least 71 lives with more than 1,000 people still reported missing, more than twice the fatalities from the 1993 Griffith Park fire in Los Angeles, which had been the deadliest.

The Camp Fire has destroyed 9,700 houses, 144 apartments and 336 commercial buildings, pretty much wiping out an entire town of more than 26,000, Paradise, about 12 miles east of Chico.

On Nov. 10, two days after the Camp Fire erupted, the president said on Twitter that “there is no reason for these massive, deadly and costly forest fires in California except that forest management is so poor. Billions of dollars are given each year, with so many lives lost, all because of gross mismanagement of the forests. Remedy now, or no more Fed payments!”

That drew a scathing response from the president of the California Professional Firefighters union that it was “ill-informed” and insensitive, and a response from Newsom that “this is not a time for partisanship.”

Brown on Friday thanked Trump for approving disaster aid and welcomed him to California, saying “Now is a time to pull together for the people of California.”

 

The cause of the Camp Fire remains undetermined. Investigators have been looking at the possibility it was ignited by electrical power lines.

Critics have noted that much of California’s forest land is federally owned. The Plumas National Forest, managed by the U.S. Forest Service, is east of Paradise in the area where the Camp Fire is believed to have started.

During the flight to California, LaMalfa visited the press cabin to remark on the significance of forest management.

“The president wants to get something done on this,” LaMalfa said, according to pool reports. “We need to be a lot more aggressive.”

LaMalfa dismissed criticism of the president’s earlier tweet on forest management as having more to do with its timing and politics than the substance of the argument. He said that forest management does not mean clear cutting but rather thinning and creating buffers around populated areas.

“Without forest management, things can go wrong,” LaMalfa said.

In Paradise, Gov. Brown said in response to a question on what needs to be done about preventing wildfires that “what needs to be done is being done.”

Trump said they had been talking about forest management on the ride over and that “I think we’re all on the same path” on that issue, he said.

“We do have to do management,” Trump said.

The President answered a few reporter questions before leaving for California, including suggesting he would round up Republican votes to get Pelosi elected speaker of the House, which Democrats won back from the GOP in the midterms.

“I like her, can you believe it?” Trump said, according to pool reports. “She’s tough and she’s smart, but she deserves to be speaker, and now they’re playing games with her … If she needs any votes, if she asks me, I will give her the votes to put her over the top.”

Asked how long U.S. troops would be guarding the southern border, where a caravan of migrant asylum seekers is headed, Trump said “as long as necessary” and questioned why the travelers seeking asylum would wave flags for their home countries.

“This has nothing to do with asylum,” the President told pool reporters, “it has to do with getting into country illegally.”
