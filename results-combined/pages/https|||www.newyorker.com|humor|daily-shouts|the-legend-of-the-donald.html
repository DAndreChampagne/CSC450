The Legend of the Donald

By Teddy Wayne

October 28, 2015

As the end of his reign neared, the king found himself without an heir, and the realm had need of a new leader. Sundry lords stepped forth for the great election, all with bountiful valor, wisdom, and chests of gold pieces. But one nobleman shone brightest with his skill at mentioning his own heroic conquests.

He was known by the people as "the Donald," and his crown was protected by an enchanted golden helmet said to be impenetrable by any sword.

Before the great election, the Donald had to vanquish a gantlet of foes in a smaller election, which would be settled in part by a battle of verbal jousting.

The Donald pointed to his nearest adversary. "Only one out of a hundred serfs is holding up a straw for him in the polls—even in his own fiefdom!" he said. "This guy's a jester."

And so the people rejoiced at his onslaught of sallies, as did those who were hosting the battle of verbal jousting, for they had never had such high attendance and could now charge spectators more gold pieces for the next contest, of which four and twenty were scheduled.

"The People to the South sneak into our realm," the Donald said. "They're bringing mead. They're taking our smithy jobs. They're rapists and pillagers. And some of them, I assume, are good serfs."

He was challenged as to how he knew that the People to the South were responsible for all these misdeeds.

"Well, somebody's doing the pillaging!" the Donald exclaimed. "I mean, somebody's doing it! Who's doing the pillaging? Who's doing the pillaging?"

Many of the people nodded; somebody was indeed doing the pillaging, and it made sense to them that it could be the People to the South. But how to keep them out of the realm?

"We need a real moat, not a toy moat like we have right now," the Donald said. "I'm talking huge alligators. And I'll get the People to the South to pay for it. Believe me, this is gonna be the classiest moat you've ever seen."

And so the people rejoiced, because the Donald had made his gold pieces building castles, which suggested he also knew everything about moats.

The Donald was celebrated, most of all by himself, for his exploits with women of unsurpassed pulchritude. The people asked his opinion of a fair maiden.

"Maybe a few harvests ago I would've wooed her with wine and song, but, sadly, she's no longer a ten-marks-on-a-shepherd's-crook," he said, shaking his head solemnly.

One of his opponents was a maiden whose visage he had disdained earlier, and the people awaited another affront.

"For the record, she's got a beautiful countenance and is a beautiful maiden," the Donald said, confounding their expectations. "I also cherish her beautiful—"

Before he could continue praising her with such courtly respect, the chief of his retinue whispered to him and suggested he stop. The people asked him about a different maiden.

"She's a sow," the Donald said.

The people beseeched him for his judgment of yet another maiden.

"Disgusting hag."

They implored him to appraise just one more.

"Revolting wench," the Donald said.

And so the people rejoiced, for although his sentiments made them cringe, he had said exactly what they secretly hoped he would say.

The Donald's reputation for building castles had inspired many apprentices who sought to learn at his feet. When apprentices displeased him, however, he would elucidate their inferior qualities and then place them inside a cannon. As an underling fired it, the Donald would declare, "You're fired—out of a cannon."

This phrase had long delighted the people, as had the other phrases he habitually employed, but the people were in a quandary. Experts in kingship had assured them that there was no harm in voting for the Donald, as he did not stand a real chance of ascending to the throne; yet these scholars were now saying, tarry, it might happen, and imagine what our realm would actually look like if we had a King the Donald!

But lo, enough people voted against the Donald in the smaller election that he did not prevail—even those who had initially supported him as a jest, or because they believed it did not matter who was king, because all lords are simply out for themselves and the monarchy is broken. The Donald was privately relieved, for in his heart of hearts he did not truly desire to be king, but was campaigning only to expand the renown of his family crest.

And so the people rejoiced, because although the victor was the lord they had originally feared would triumph, he at least seemed to have a general idea of what the kingship entailed and, after so many amusing divertissements in the smaller election, the people had nearly forgotten about the disastrous reign, several harvests ago, of the older brother in his family's dynasty.

  • Teddy Wayne is the author of three novels, most recently “Loner.”
