Donald Trump is the 45th president of the United States.

On June 16, 2015, Trump announced his bid for the presidency at Trump Tower in New York City. Trump said, "I am officially running for president of the United States, and we are going to make our country great again."[1]

Biography

Donald John Trump was born in Queens, New York. His father, Frederick C. Trump, was a real estate developer who built apartments in Queens, Staten Island and Brooklyn. His mother, Mary MacLeod Trump, was a Scottish immigrant.[2] Donald Trump attended the Kew-Forest School in Queens before his parents sent him to the New York Military Academy in Cornwall-on-Hudson at age 13. Trump played three varsity sports at the military school and graduated in 1964 with academic honors.[3]

Trump attended Fordham University for two years before transferring to the Wharton School at the University of Pennsylvania. He earned his bachelor of science degree in economics in 1968.[2] During college, Trump spent his summers working for his father. After graduation, he joined his father’s company.[4]

Career

Real estate

When Trump joined his father’s company, Elizabeth Trump and Son, after college, he urged his father to take on more debt and to expand the business in Manhattan.[4] Donald Trump took control of the company in 1971, and later renamed it the Trump Organization. One of Trump’s first successful ventures in New York City was his purchase of the Commodore Hotel from the Penn Central Railroad in 1974. In 1980, after six years of renovation, the 34-story Grand Hyatt Hotel opened for business.[5] Trump broke ground on Trump Tower in 1980. The mostly residential, 48-story luxury high-rise opened in 1983.[5]

Atlantic City casinos

In 1984, Trump ventured outside New York City to develop the first of three gambling casinos in Atlantic City, New Jersey, Harrah's at Trump Plaza (later Trump Plaza). A second casino, Trump’s Castle, in Atlantic City’s marina district, opened in 1985. In the late 1980s, Trump took over construction of a boardwalk hotel and casino: the Taj Mahal. The Trump Taj Mahal, the largest and most expensive casino at the time, opened in April 1990. After 15 months in operation, the Taj filed for Chapter 11 bankruptcy protection. Trump's casino companies also filed for bankruptcy in 1992, 2004 and 2009. This was a period of decline for Atlantic City casinos due to a slow economy and increased competition in nearby states.[6] Trump Plaza closed in September 2014. Under a bankruptcy restructuring plan, the Taj Mahal remains open under new ownership.[7]

Reality television

Trump hosted and served as executive producer of “The Apprentice” on NBC from 2004 to 2015 when NBC severed all business ties with Trump.[5][8] The reality television game show tests contestants’ business skills as they competed to become Trump’s apprentice.[9] The show spurred the catch-phrase, “You’re fired!” and inspired the spinoff, “The Celebrity Apprentice.”[10]

Author

Trump is the author of 15 books, including his 1987 memoir, “The Art of the Deal.”[11]

Other business ventures

Trump owns and operates golf courses in California, Florida, New Jersey, New York, North Carolina, Washington, DC, and in Ireland, Scotland and the United Arab Emirates.[12] He also owns Wollman Rink, New York City’s Central Park ice rink, and Trump Place, a housing development with a 5,700 apartments along the Hudson River.[5] In 1988, Trump bought Eastern Air Shuttle, an airline service that ran hourly flights between Boston, New York City and Washington, DC, and transformed it into a luxury experience. The shuttle ceased operation in 1992 after Trump was forced to turn it over to creditors.[5] In the mid-2000s, Trump launched his own vodka, premium steaks and magazine; each was discontinued.[5]

Wave elections (1918-2016)


See also: Wave elections (1918-2016)

The term wave election is frequently used to describe an election cycle in which one party makes significant electoral gains. How many seats would Republicans have to lose for the 2018 midterm election to be considered a wave election?

Ballotpedia examined the results of the 50 election cycles that occurred between 1918 and 2016—spanning from President Woodrow Wilson's (D) second midterm in 1918 to Donald Trump's (R) first presidential election in 2016. We define wave elections as the 20 percent of elections in that period resulting in the greatest seat swings against the president's party.

Applying this definition to four different election groups (U.S. Senate, U.S. House, governorships, and state legislatures) yields specific numbers of seats that Republicans would need to lose for 2018 to qualify as a wave election. Those are:

  • 48 U.S. House seats,
  • Seven U.S. Senate seats,
  • Seven gubernatorial seats, or
  • 494 state legislative seats.

According to NPR’s analysis of data from the Center for Responsive Politics, donations to national-level parties, candidates, and other committees made in Donald Trump's name since 1989 have totaled around an inflation-adjusted $1.4 million, with approximately two-thirds going to Republicans. From 1989 to 2009, more than half of Trump’s donations went to Democrats, but from 2010 to 2015, 97 percent of his donations went to Republicans. Trump has made more than 40 percent of his nearly $1.4 million in donations since 2010.[13]

Reform Party

During the 2000 presidential primaries, Trump made an effort to receive the nomination of the Reform Party. Trump joined the Reform Party on October 25, 1999, and sought to secure the party’s nomination during the 2000 presidential primaries. Trump presented himself as an alternative to then front-runner, Pat Buchanan. Trump participated in the California primary and received 15,311 votes. He then withdrew his candidacy, voicing concerns about the state of the Reform Party.[3]

Evolving politics

During the last two decades, Trump has been a Republican, an independent, a Democrat and a Republican again.[14] Between 1987 and 2012, Trump changed his official party affiliation six times, registering most recently as a Republican in April 2012.[15]

See also: Donald Trump presidential campaign, 2016

Personal

Trump has five children and seven grandchildren and has been married three times.[16] Trump married his first wife, Ivana, a former model and Olympic skier from Czechoslovakia, in 1977. The couple had three children, Donald Jr., Ivanka and Eric. Ivana Trump was vice president of interior design for the Trump Organization and was responsible for the interior design of the Grand Hyatt Hotel, Trump Tower and the Trump Plaza Hotel and Casino in Atlantic City. The couple separated in 1990 and divorced in 1992.[17]

Tiffany Trump, is the daughter of Donald Trump and Marla Maples. Trump and Maples, then an aspiring actress, married in 1993 and divorced in 1999.[18]

Trump married his third wife, Melania, a Slovenian born model, in 2005. The couple has one son, Barron Trump.[19] They reside in a three-story penthouse atop Trump Tower in Manhattan.[20] Trump also has numerous apartments in Manhattan and estates in Palm Beach, Florida; Bedford, New York; Beverly Hills, California and rural Virginia.[21]

Noteworthy events

Federal judge denies Trump's motion to dismiss emoluments suit

On July 25, 2018, a federal judge denied Trump's motion to dismiss a lawsuit alleging that he had violated the Foreign and Domestic Emoluments Clauses of the United States Constitution. Judge Peter Messitte ruled that the plaintiffs, the attorneys general for Maryland and the District of Columbia, had "plausibly alleged that the President has been receiving or is potentially able to receive 'emoluments' from foreign, the federal and state governments in violation of the Constitution." Messitte ordered the parties to the suit to submit a joint recommendation to the court within 21 days indicating the next steps to be taken in the case.[22]

Messitte's ruling was the first made by a federal court establishing a definition of emolument. Messitte defined emolument as any "'profit,' 'gain,' or 'advantage.'" Messitte rejected the definition proffered by the United States Department of Justice, which construed an emolument as "a payment made in connection with a particular employment over and above one's salary."[23][24]

The Foreign Emoluments Clause stipulates that "no Person holding any Office of Profit or Trust under them, shall, without the Consent of the Congress, accept of any present, Emolument, Office, or Title, of any kind whatever, from any King, Prince, or foreign State." The Domestic Emoluments Clause provides that the president "shall not receive within that Period [i.e., the term of office] any other Emolument from the United States, or any of them."[25][26]

Sexual misconduct allegations

See also: Sexual assault and harassment in American politics (2017-2018)

Donald Trump was mentioned during a wave of sexual assault and misconduct allegations in 2017 and 2018. During the 2016 presidential campaign, at least 16 women accused Trump of sexual misconduct following the release of a recording from Access Hollywood where Trump spoke about grabbing women without their consent. Trump apologized for the comments in the tape and said he never engaged in the activities he described. According to White House Press Secretary Sarah Huckabee Sanders on October 27, 2017, the official White House position was that the allegations were not true.[27][28]
