    PITTSBURGH —

    President Donald Trump and first lady Melania Trump will travel to Pittsburgh on Tuesday in the wake of the Squirrel Hill synagogue massacre that left 11 people dead.

    Air Force One is scheduled to arrive at Pittsburgh International Airport at 3:45 p.m., according to a statement released by the White House Monday evening.

    Advertisement

    White House spokeswoman Sarah Huckabee Sanders said Trump is coming to the city "to express the support of the American people and to grieve with the Pittsburgh community." No other details or an itinerary for the visit were immediately announced.

    Mayor Bill Peduto told reporters ahead of the announcement that the White House should consult with the families of the victims about their preferences and asked that the president not come during a funeral. Services for three of the victims will be held Tuesday.

    "If the President is looking to come to Pittsburgh, I would ask that he not do so while we are burying the dead. Our attention and our focus is going to be on them, and we don't have public safety that we can take away from what is needed in order to do both," Peduto said.

    As a mark of solemn respect for the victims at The Tree of Life Synagogue in Pittsburgh, PA, POTUS issued a Proclamation that the flag of the United States be flown at half-staff at the White House, at all military posts & naval stations & on all naval vessels of the Federal Govt pic.twitter.com/ltA3Lemd3u

    Eleven people were fatally shot at Tree of Life on Saturday morning. Six more, including four police officers, were wounded.

    The suspect, 46-year-old Robert Bowers, made his first appearance in federal court Monday. He was ordered to remain in the Allegheny County Jail pending his preliminary hearing, which is set for 10 a.m. Thursday.

    Trump has condemned the shooting as an "evil anti-Semitic attack." He ordered flags at federal buildings throughout the country to be flown at half-staff until Wednesday in "solemn respect" for the victims.

    At an Illinois rally Saturday night, hours after the mass shooting, Trump said "the hearts of all Americans are filled with grief, following the monstrous killing." He also sought to distance himself from Bowers, calling him "sick" and saying "he was no supporter of mine."

    All of America is in mourning over the mass murder of Jewish Americans at the Tree of Life Synagogue in Pittsburgh. We pray for those who perished and their loved ones, and our hearts go out to the brave police officers who sustained serious injuries...

    — Donald J. Trump (@realDonaldTrump) October 27, 2018

    Earlier in the day, Trump speculated that the death toll would have been curbed if the synagogue had armed security. Bowers was eventually subdued by SWAT teams who entered the building and shot the gunman.

    "This is a case where, if they had an armed guard inside, they might have been able to stop him immediately," Trump said. "Maybe there would have been nobody killed, except for him, frankly. So it's a very, very -- a very difficult situation."

    "The world is a violent world," he said before his speech. "And you think when you're over it, it just sort of goes away, but then it comes back in the form of a madman, a wacko. ... They had a maniac walk in and they didn't have any protection and that is just so sad to see, so sad to see."

    Trump said lawmakers "should very much bring the death penalty into vogue" and people who kill in places such as synagogues and churches "really should suffer the ultimate price."
