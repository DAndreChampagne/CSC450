Following the Donald Trump workout

  • Share via e-mail

  • Print
  • 46 Comments
46
By Alex Beam   September 24, 2018

Only an idiot would take Donald Trump’s absurd ideas about exercise and human metabolism seriously.

I am that idiot.

Bystanders first observed Trump’s entropy at a Group of Seven summit in Sicily last year. Trump used a golf cart to travel a few hundred yards, while the other heads of state hoofed it. This reluctance to walk, likewise a hallmark of his lazy man’s golf game, turns out to be part of Trump’s “battery theory” of the human body.

Advertisement

He believes that, like a motor or a jet engine, the heart has only so many cycles in a lifetime. (For a jet, this calculation is called “mean time between failures.”) So Trump views walking, or exercise of almost any kind, as an unnecessary strain on the system.

“All my friends who work out all the time, they’re going for knee replacements, hip replacements,” Trump told the New York Times Magazine. “They’re a disaster.”

With one hip replacement in my rearview mirror and heaven knows what bionic interventions still to come, I, like Trump, have soured on the American cult of exercise. My younger self ran and played basketball, tennis, and squash. Now I’m just a sitting duck for any orthopedic surgeon who needs to make a quick $20,000 before tee time.

I wish someone had told me: Walk a mile a day, do your errands on your bike, eat and drink in moderation, and you’ll live to be 1,000.

But no one did.

Advertisement

My internist once explained to me that the heart muscle is not like, say, a quad or biceps, meaning that it cannot be enlarged or strengthened. This is not entirely true. The heart muscle is indeed different from skeletal muscles, but it can be strengthened.

Dr. Sandeep Jauhar , a cardiologist who just published “Heart: A History,” explains that heart cells cannot multiply like skeletal cells, but they can grow larger and stronger, with exercise. Trump’s metabolic notions “are absolutely ridiculous; they have no basis in fact,” he said. Exercise generally “improves the heart’s pumping function and improves overall health.”

Or does it? “Exercise may provoke problems such as myocardial ischemia, dysrhythmias, and even sudden cardiac death in individuals with significant (possibly silent) coronary heart disease,” longtime Harvard Medical School professor Dr. Arthur Siegel wrote in the premonitory foreword to one of my favorite books, “Why I Don’t Jog, Play Tennis, Bicycle, Swim, Row, or Dance,” by Thomas McCann.

“Jog” is the fruit of McCann’s “morbid hobby of collecting the obituaries of people who died while exercising. Each of these documented deaths, which quickly filled a fat file,” the sedentary 84-year-old McCann wrote, “only reassured me in a perverse way that I had found the key to a long and healthy life.”

The all-time classic jogging obituary belongs to 52-year-old running evangelist and fitness promoter James Fixx, who died while jogging in Vermont in 1984. The New York Times dryly noted that Fixx “preached the gospel that active people live longer.” Sadly, he was not one of them.

Advertisement

National Public Radio recently reported that mollusks with higher metabolic rates became extinct more quickly than their torpid counterparts, according to a study in the impressive-sounding “Proceedings of the Royal Society B.” Reporter Laurel Wamsley said that the new research “suggests one effective evolutionary strategy: “Be lazy.”

God knows I am trying! I do walk, and I do perform my errands on my bike. I wish nothing but happy, healthy hearts and unreplaced joints for all: for corpulent, accident-of-history presidents; for talented authors (read Jauhar’s excellent “Doctored: The Disillusionment of an American Physician”); for my friends Siegel and McCann; and for mollusks and bivalves the world over.
